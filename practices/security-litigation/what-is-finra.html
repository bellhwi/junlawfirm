What is FINRA?

The Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) is a non-governmental organization that oversees U.S. brokerage firms and their registered representatives. Its primary role is to protect investors by ensuring the securities industry operates fairly and honestly. FINRA is authorized by the U.S. Congress and operates under the supervision of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

How Can FINRA Help Protect Investors?

FINRA provides several key functions and services aimed at protecting investors and maintaining the integrity of the financial markets:

1. Regulation and Oversight

Licensing and Registration: FINRA oversees the licensing and registration of brokerage firms and individual brokers. It ensures that those who sell securities are qualified and adhere to ethical standards.

Compliance Inspections: FINRA conducts regular examinations of brokerage firms to ensure compliance with federal securities laws and FINRA rules.

Rule Enforcement: FINRA enforces rules designed to protect investors and ensure fair and honest markets. These rules cover various aspects of brokerage operations, including suitability, communications, and trading practices.

2. Investor Education

Resources and Tools: FINRA offers a wealth of educational resources to help investors make informed decisions. This includes information on investment products, risks, and strategies.

BrokerCheck: This free tool allows investors to research the background and disciplinary history of brokerage firms and individual brokers. It provides crucial information about an advisorâ€™s qualifications and any past misconduct.

3. Dispute Resolution

  • Arbitration and Mediation: FINRA provides arbitration and mediation services to resolve disputes between investors and brokerage firms or brokers. These services offer a more efficient and cost-effective alternative to litigation.

  • Arbitration: A formal process where a neutral arbitrator or panel hears both sides of the dispute and makes a binding decision.

  • Mediation: A voluntary process where a neutral mediator helps the parties negotiate a mutually acceptable settlement.

4. Market Transparency and Integrity

Market Surveillance: FINRA monitors trading activity to detect and prevent fraudulent practices, such as insider trading, market manipulation, and other abuses.

Trade Reporting: FINRA operates systems like TRACE (Trade Reporting and Compliance Engine) to increase transparency in bond markets by requiring firms to report their transactions.

5. Regulatory Notices and Alerts

Investor Alerts: FINRA issues alerts to warn investors about potential scams, high-risk investment products, and market events that could impact their investments.

Regulatory Notices: These notices inform firms about changes in rules, compliance requirements, and other important regulatory updates.

Specific Protections for Investors

  1. Suitability Rule (FINRA Rule 2111): Requires brokers to have a reasonable basis for believing that a recommended transaction or investment strategy is suitable for the customer based on their investment profile.

  1. Anti-Fraud Rule (FINRA Rule 2020): Prohibits brokers from using any manipulative, deceptive, or other fraudulent devices or contrivances in the course of their business.
